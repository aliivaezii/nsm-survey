How Organizations Select Their North Star Metric

A Mixed-Methods Decision Framework for Growth Hacking Metric Selection

This survey is part of an academic research project at Politecnico di Torino and the University of Turin. We study how growth-oriented organizations evaluate and select their primary performance metric, the "North Star Metric." Your responses are fully anonymous and will be used solely for research purposes.

8–12 minutes Fully anonymous Academic research only

Criteria for Selecting a Growth Metric

Rate how important each criterion is when selecting a North Star Metric. Scale: 1 = Not at all important → 7 = Extremely important.

C1. Value Alignment

The metric directly reflects the core value your product delivers to customers.

Not at all important Extremely important
C2. Measurability

The metric can be tracked reliably and frequently enough to guide weekly decisions.

Not at all important Extremely important
C3. Actionability

Teams can directly influence this metric through experiments and initiatives.

Not at all important Extremely important
C4. Predictive Power

Short-term changes in this metric reliably signal long-term business growth.

Not at all important Extremely important
C5. Team Alignment

The metric creates a shared focus that unifies marketing, product, and engineering.

Not at all important Extremely important
C6. Gaming Resistance

The metric is hard to manipulate or inflate artificially when it becomes a target.

Not at all important Extremely important
C7. Experiment Sensitivity

The metric is sensitive enough to detect the impact of individual experiments.

Not at all important Extremely important
C8. Strategic Fit

The metric aligns with the organization's current growth stage and strategic direction.
